# Limber — Capstone Opening Script
### Presented by Deepti Hemdev · ~3 minutes (~430 words) · target ~130 wpm
> Reviewed by a 4-professor panel (rhetoric, neuroscience, business, assessment); their fixes are incorporated. Deliver the pauses — they are the punctuation.

---

**[STEP FORWARD. SCAN THE ROOM. ONE BEAT OF SILENCE BEFORE YOU SPEAK.]**

**[0:00 — THE HOOK]**

A company once told millions of people that a video game would make them smarter.

**[PAUSE]**

The FTC — the U.S. Federal Trade Commission — fined them two million dollars.

**[PAUSE]**

That company was Lumosity. And they weren't fined for lying — they were fined for selling a claim the science couldn't back.

**[0:25 — THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH]**

Here's the uncomfortable truth about brain-training apps. You can get very good at the game. You do not get better at life.

Train on a grid of flashing tiles, and you get better at that grid — and tasks just like it. But your memory at work? Your focus? Your ability to learn something new? There's no reliable evidence they improve. Scientists call it the absence of *"far transfer"* — and across the rigorous studies, the effect is essentially zero.

This is a five-to-seven-billion-dollar industry. And most of the science says it doesn't work the way it's being sold.

**[PAUSE]**

So what happens when an entire category is built on overclaiming? People try it. They feel a little sharper for a week. And then they leave — research shows roughly **seventy percent quit within a hundred days.** Not because people don't want to change. Because the product misled them about how change actually works.

**[1:25 — THE PIVOT]**

But here's what's also true.

Your brain *is* plastic — genuinely, throughout your life. The catch is that this capacity gets harder to unlock as we age, and it was never effortless to begin with. Because real plasticity is *specific.* It's *slow.* And it takes *work.*

We've known how it happens for decades. Kleim and Jones laid out the principles — specificity, intensity, repetition. And the levers are unglamorous: focused practice, sleep, exercise, managing stress.

Every single feature I built maps to one of those principles. That's the entire design.

**[2:05 — THE THESIS]**

My product is called Limber. And its core advantage is something almost no one in this space will attempt.

**[PAUSE]**

Telling the truth.

**[PAUSE — LET IT LAND]**

In a market built on overclaiming, honesty is the moat.

**[PAUSE]**

A moat — the one advantage a competitor can't copy. And ours is *real*: a rival can clone our features in a weekend, but they can't clone our honesty without giving up the overclaims their business runs on. The experts here trust no app yet — and whoever earns that trust first owns it.

Limber trains your brain the way it actually changes — and refuses every claim the evidence won't support.

**[2:45 — HAND OFF]**

Here's what I'll show you: the daily loop, the science beneath it, the business, and why trust is the thing that finally wins this category.

**[PAUSE]**

Let's begin.

**[ADVANCE SLIDE.]**

## Delivery cheat-sheet
- **The sticky line:** *"In a market built on overclaiming, honesty is the moat."* Slow down. Full stop before it, held beat after. Don't bury it.
- **Two non-negotiable pauses:** after "two million dollars," and after "Telling the truth."
- **Pace:** ~130 wpm. The script is ~430 words; with the pauses it lands right at 3:00. If you run fast, the pauses absorb the slack — don't add words.
- **Energy map:** cool and factual through the hook → controlled tension in the middle → warm conviction at "honesty is the moat" → brisk and confident on the hand-off.
- **Eye contact:** land the hook on one person; open up to the room at "But here's what's also true."

## Panel fixes incorporated
1. **Market figure** corrected to **$5–7B (brain-training software)** — consistent with your own dossier (the one number a faculty member could otherwise falsify from your submission).
2. **Science made Q&A-proof:** "far transfer ≈ zero" (precise, not "pretty much null"); "no reliable evidence broader memory/focus improve" (not "unchanged" — respects near-transfer); plasticity "throughout life, harder with age" (respects the age nuance); Lumosity reframed as "a claim the science couldn't back" (the FTC's actual finding).
3. **"Honesty is the moat" rescued** — its own line + held pause (rhetoric panel).
4. **Moat defended structurally** — first-mover credibility / expert distrust / lock-in a copycat can't buy (business panel).
5. **Science → product** — "every feature maps to a Kleim & Jones principle" signals command, not name-dropping (assessment panel).
